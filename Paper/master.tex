	\begin{center}
		\section*{Summary}
	\end{center}
As global electricity grids face increasing strain from electric vehicles, data 
centres, and large-scale industrial consumption, the volatility of electricity 
prices has become a pressing financial concern for energy-intensive businesses. 
In Denmark, where more than 80\% of electricity is generated by 
weather-dependent renewable sources such as wind and solar power, accurate price
forecasting is essential for minimising 
operational costs. Energy trading 
companies and industrial consumers stand to benefit significantly from 
predicting prices on the day-ahead market, allowing them to schedule heavy 
consumption during periods of high renewable output and low cost.

To achieve this, academic literature has proposed a variety of short-term 
electricity price forecasting models. However, much of this prior work focuses 
on highly specialised, computationally expensive 
deep learning architectures 
trained on fixed, heavily curated datasets. While these models achieve strong 
academic benchmarks, they represent a significant investment in both
development and computational maintenance. For an industry practitioner, this 
obscures a critical operational question: how much data and architectural 
complexity is actually required to build a commercially viable forecasting 
system?

This thesis addresses this gap through a comprehensive, large-scale ablation 
study tailored to the Danish energy market. Rather than chasing peak predictive 
performance through exhaustive engineering, the study evaluates the baseline 
capabilities of standard, out-of-the-box machine learning models. A total of 
1,052 models were trained across eight distinct architectures, encompassing 
tree-based models, specifically RandomForest, XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost, 
neural networks, LSTM, GRU, Transformer, and an automatic machine learning 
ensemble framework by the name of AutoGluon.

The experimental design systematically varied three core components. First, 
models were trained on 13 progressively expanding feature sets. These ranged 
from raw weather and time data to extensive combinations of historical grid 
exchange and pricing data. This was done to isolate the marginal predictive 
value of each data category. Second, the forecasting horizon was
sequentially expanded from 0-hours up to 168-hours, to observe performance 
degradation. Finally, models were evaluated on two distinct targets: the 
absolute electricity price and the price delta, to determine if behaviour 
differed depending on whether the models were instructed to predict the 
absolute price, or simply the change in price. To ensure robustness and prevent 
data leakage over the 11-year dataset, all models were evaluated using an 
expanding-window walk-forward validation technique.

The results of the ablation study yield clear, actionable directives for 
industry. First, tree-based models, and CatBoost in particular, consistently 
outperformed neural network architectures at short forecasting horizons when 
using default configurations. While the performance gap narrowed at longer 
horizons, tree-based models proved more resilient and significantly faster to
train. Second, the feature contribution analysis revealed that historical price 
data is the single most critical variable for accurate prediction. While 
weather and time encodings serve as a necessary baseline, the continuous 
addition of complex features, such as lagged electricity production and 
consumption metrics, resulted in  diminishing returns and, in some cases, 
degraded model performance.

Supplementary experiments validated these findings and provided further 
operational insight. While targeted hyperparameter tuning via Optuna yielded 
substantial improvements, specifically improving \gls{mae} by up to 33\% across 
the tree-based models, substituting the project’s custom-made, aggregated, 
station-level weather data with professionally processed data from our 
commercial partner Midas Energy provided no measurable predictive advantage. 

Ultimately, this thesis demonstrates that companies, depending on their 
accuracy needs, do not need to rely on excessively complex neural networks or 
exhaustive data acquisition pipelines to forecast electricity prices 
effectively. A reasonably competitive, robust forecasting system can be 
deployed using standard, computationally efficient tree-based algorithms paired
with a constrained dataset of weather, time, and historical prices.

\begin{abstract}
  Short-term electricity price forecasting is critical for
  industrial energy planning and grid stability. However, existing
  literature often focuses on computationally expensive, highly
  specialised architectures tested on fixed 
  feature sets. This leaves industry practitioners without clear guidance 
  regarding whether the acquisition of varied data and increased model 
  complexity is justified. This thesis presents a large-scale ablation study of 
  the Danish 
  day-ahead 
  electricity market, systematically evaluating 1,052 default-configured 
  machine 
  learning models across eight architectures.
  These include tree-based models, neural networks, and AutoGluon.
  The models are assessed using walk-forward validation across 13
  progressively expanding feature constellations, eight forecasting
  horizons, ranging from 0-hours to 168-hours, and two prediction
  targets: absolute price and price delta. Results demonstrate
  that general-purpose tree-based models, particularly CatBoost,
  dominate short-term forecasting horizons, outperforming default-configured 
  neural networks. Feature contribution analysis reveals
  that historical price data is the most critical predictive feature,
  whereas expanding the dataset to include lagged grid consumption
  metrics yields diminishing or negative returns. Supplementary
  experiments demonstrate that targeted hyperparameter tuning
  improves MAE by up to 33\%. Additionally, substituting raw
  station-level weather data with professionally processed commercial weather 
  data yields no significant predictive advantage.
  These findings provide actionable guidance for the industry:
  respectable forecasting accuracy can be achieved using out-of-the-box 
  algorithms and a constrained feature set consisting of weather,
  time, and historical prices, effectively avoiding the operational
  overhead associated with specialised deep 
  learning architectures
  and exhaustive data acquisition and maintenance.
\end{abstract}
